introduction

Dying Behind Bars: Beyond the Data is based off a dataset compiled by the news organization Reuters documenting deaths in U.S. local jails between 2008-2019. Released at the end of 2020, Reuters’ examination of deaths in local jails represents the largest collection and publication of data on inmate mortality ever done outside of the federal government. This Reuters data is remarkable because it highlights the failures of our government on many levels, some of which we will explore throughout this project as we start to uncover the context in which this dataset sits.

While Dying Behind Bars is a data visualization project, it is, at its core, a humanistic endeavor meant to illuminate the fact that these are human beings who deserve to be remembered as more than a statistic or data point.

We will be focusing specifically on female jail deaths in the state of California, which is the state with by far the most jail deaths in the country for both men and women. The 119 female deaths in CA represent about 1.5% of the 7,571 total jail deaths throughout the nation that are included in the Reuters data. Here is a breakdown of the number of jail deaths by state and gender:

Here we see that for female deaths throughout the country, CA has the most out of any state. CA's 119 female jail deaths represents 13% of the 909 total female jail deaths nationwide.

This project is inspired by the women leading the abolition feminism movement who have been working to highlight carceral realities for decades and whose words and ideas are referenced throughout. These women include Angela Y. Davis, Kelly Lytle Hernandez, Gina Dent, Ruth Wilson Gilmore, Mariame Kaba, Joanne Barker, and Michelle Alexander.
 
The project also works from the understanding that carceral punishment and racial capitalism are directly connected; while this is not necessarily the focus here or explicitly evident through the dataset or the visualizations presented, this is the knowledge from which all aspects of this project grew and continues to grow. This project is very much a work in progress.

Dying Behind Bars: Beyond the Data adopts as its guiding light the notion that data is false completeness. Who and what gets erased in data relating to mass incarceration? The project takes up, in its own humble and limited way, the call from historian, scholar, and UCLA professor Kelly Lytle Hernandez to think beyond facticity. While data undoubtedly has power, we must also recognize its limitations in conveying the deep damage, harm, and pain perpetuated by a system that continues to benefit from mass incarceration and overinvest in a military police state at the expense of human lives.

Hernandez urges us to think about the stories that need to be told as we collectively adopt an abolitionist mindset and take the necessary next steps in making investments towards safety, security, and wellbeing through secure housing, family support services, and mental health intervention and support.
Data does have a role in the story, but it is not the whole story.
